Building a Product Strategy

Overview of Product Strategy

A Product Strategy is a broad plan that outlines a company's goals and methods for achieving those goals with its product. It is the process of defining what you want to achieve and how you plan to get there. It outlines your strategic vision for a product offering by stating where the product is going, how it will get there, and why it will succeed.

Let us get started with a video that will provide a comprehensive understanding of What is Product Strategy, Why it is important, and How can you align your Product Strategy.

A Product Strategy answers key questions such as who the product will serve, how it will benefit them, and how it will further the company's goals throughout the product's lifecycle. It enables you to focus on a specific target market and feature set, instead of trying to be everything to everyone.

Defining a Product Strategy

A good Product Strategy guides all product-related decisions, from design and engineering to marketing and sales. To create a winning Product Strategy, you need a deep understanding of the market, competition, customer needs, and the business's strengths and weaknesses. A successful Product Strategy creates a comprehensive plan that outlines how a company, or team, will deliver value to users, set itself apart from the competition, and drive business impact.

A well-defined Product Strategy includes several key elements:

  • Product Vision
  • Insights
  • Challenges
  • Approaches
  • Accountability

In today's rapidly evolving market, a strong Product Strategy is essential for companies to remain competitive, achieve their goals, and meet the evolving needs of their customers.

You can’t have Product Strategy without Product Vision or Company Vision/Mission/Goals.

Crafting a Product Strategy in 6 Steps

Forming a Product Strategy requires a deep understanding of the market, customer needs, and business objectives. It consists of 6 major steps. Click on each of the steps in the interactive image below to learn more.

Step 1: Market Analysis

Market analysis is a critical component of Product Strategy development. This step involves gathering and analyzing data on the market, including customer needs, behaviors, and pain points, as well as competitive offerings and industry trends. The purpose of market analysis is to gain a deep understanding of the market landscape and identify opportunities for your product to succeed.

Let us look at a video explaining what market analysis is and the steps involved in the process.

Before focusing on starting with a market analysis, make sure you design your analysis to address the following items:

  • Who are my potential users?
  • What purchasing habits do my users have?
  • What size is my intended market?
  • What prices are my users willing to pay?
  • Who are my primary competitors?
  • What are the advantages and disadvantages of my competitors?

Steps in Market Analysis

Market Analysis Benefits

Market analysis is important because it provides a deep understanding of the market landscape and identifies opportunities for the product to succeed. By understanding customer needs and preferences, the product can be designed and developed to better meet those needs. This can help improve customer satisfaction, loyalty, and retention, leading to increased revenue and profitability over time.

There are several benefits of conducting market analysis, including:

  • Improved decision-making: By understanding the market landscape and identifying opportunities for the product to succeed, market analysis can help improve decision-making and reduce the risk of failure.
  • Targeted product development: By segmenting the market and understanding customer needs, market analysis can help guide product development and ensure that the product meets the needs of its target market.
  • Competitive advantage: By understanding the competitive landscape and identifying gaps in the market, market analysis can help the product differentiate itself from competitors and provide a unique value prop.
  • Improved communication: By understanding customer needs and pain points, market analysis can help improve communication with customers, teams, and other stakeholders, leading to better alignment and collaboration.
  • Increased revenue: By identifying opportunities for the product to succeed and improving customer satisfaction and retention, market analysis can lead to increased revenue and profitability over time.

Step 2: Customer Analytics

We are now switching gears and diving into customer analytics, which is a critical component of market analysis and Product Strategy development. Customer analytics is a careful examination of the data a business gathers on its customers. Teams can use customer analytics to identify, attract, and retain customers to drive growth.

This process involves identifying the target customer segments and understanding their needs, behaviors, and preferences. The purpose of customer analytics is to gain a deep understanding of the customer and develop a product that meets their needs and solves their problems.

Steps involved in Customer Analytics:

1. Identifying target customer segments

The first step in customer analytics is to identify the target customer segments for the product. This can be done using demographic, psychographic, and behavioral data, as well as market research and competitive analysis.

First of all, determine whether your market is viable and substantial. After that, divide the market into groups of customers with similar tastes and spending patterns. The car market, for instance, is divided into various segmented divisions, first by gender, age, class, etc.

2. Segment the customer base

As soon as the target customer segments have been identified, they can be segmented into smaller groups based on common needs, preferences, and behaviors. This allows for a more targeted and effective Product Strategy.

Some useful questions to consider when segmenting your customer base are:

  • What are your customers buying habits and preferences?
  • Who makes the purchasing decision?
  • How frequently do they buy a product?
  • What incentivizes a purchasing decision?

After that, concentrate your marketing efforts on showing them how your product and service will fit into their lifestyle and best satisfy their demands.

3. Gather Data

The next step in customer analytics is to gather data on the target customer segments. At each stage of your process, ask your clients for input to determine their needs and preferences. There are several approaches to determining the demands of your customers. Click on the tabs in the images below to explore further.

4. Analyze the data

After gathering data, develop customer insights and communicate your results to your colleagues. Utilize these insights to spot possibilities to modify your products or services—or ways that you describe them on your website—better in order to facilitate conversions more easily.

The goal is to replace quicker marketing efforts with more proactive, strategic, and data-driven ones by conducting this kind of research and routinely sharing the findings with your team.

5. Develop customer personas

The final step in customer analytics is to develop customer personas. This involves creating detailed profiles of the target customer segments, including their goals, challenges, preferences, and behaviors.

Customer personas, also known as user personas, are fictitious, composite characters that represent a subset of your customers and are created using data from market research. They clearly depict subsets of key user segments and can help product teams and stakeholders better understand and empathize with user mindsets and needs.

Customer Analytics Benefits

Customer analytics is important because it provides a deep understanding of the target customer and their needs. By understanding customer needs and pain points, the product can be designed and developed to better meet those needs. This can help improve customer satisfaction, loyalty, and retention, leading to increased revenue and profitability over time.

Step 4: OKRs

Let’s have a look at how to craft solid OKRs for our Product Strategy. OKR stands for ‘Objectives and Key Results,’ and this is a goal-setting formula that ensures everyone is working on tasks aligned with the greater strategy. Setting OKRs when developing a Product Strategy can help to guarantee that your team is focused, accountable, and adaptable.

Let us take a look at a video explaining OKRs, how to create effective OKRs, benefits of OKRs and an example.

Steps for creating OKRs

An Example of OKRs

Your objective is to increase user engagement on your mobile app

Your Key results are to:

  • Increase daily active users by 20% within the next quarter
  • Increase average session duration by 10% within the next quarter
  • Increase the number of user reviews by 50% within the next quarter

Your Targets are to :

  • Increase daily active users from 10,000 to 12,000 within the next quarter
  • Increase average session duration from 5 minutes to 5.5 minutes within the next quarter
  • Increase the number of user reviews from 100 to 150 within the next quarter

Ownership: Product Manager and Mobile Development team

Keep in mind that good OKRs ought to be clear, quantifiable, doable, pertinent, and time-bound. These procedures will enable you to develop OKRs that support your Product Strategy and advance the realization of your product vision.

Benefits of OKRs

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) offer several benefits for Product Strategy:

  • Focus: OKRs help define precise, quantifiable goals aligned with your overall Product Strategy. Clear objectives ensure your team concentrates on the most important goals for your product
  • Alignment: OKRs provide a common language and understanding for the product development team. When everyone agrees on the objectives and key results, collaboration and joint effort towards a common goal become easier
  • Accountability: Clear, measurable targets and key results enable you to hold team members responsible for goal attainment. This ensures that everyone works towards the same objectives and achieves them
  • Flexibility and adaptability: OKRs allow for adjustments to reflect changes in your product and business goals. They help ensure your Product Strategy remains in line with your broader business plan

Crafting a Game-changing Product Strategy

Great product companies have four things in common: a clear product vision; a Product Strategy; a set of priorities; and a way to measure outcomes. Let us take a look at each of them in more details. Click on the tabs below to expand them further.

Product Vision

A well-defined product vision is crucial for ensuring that your product remains focused and on track to achieve its desired outcomes. It serves as the foundation for all product decisions and activities.

Importance of Product Vision

  • Aligns team members and stakeholders towards a shared understanding of the product’s purpose and goals
  • Serves as a guiding force that inspires and motivates the team to achieve their objectives
  • Is adaptable and flexible, allowing for adjustments based on market changes, customer feedback, and evolving business objectives.

Developing a Compelling Vision

To craft a compelling product vision, start by identifying the core purpose of your product, focusing on the value it brings to customers and the problems it solves. Be specific about your target audience and their needs.

Strategic Differentiation

Clearly articulate how your product stands out from competitors and what makes it unique. This will help guide your team in making decisions that align with your product's strategic advantages.

Time Horizon

Define a realistic time frame for achieving your product vision, taking into account the current market landscape, technology advancements, and potential challenges.

Communicating the Vision

Share your product vision with the entire team, stakeholders, and even customers to ensure alignment, generate excitement, and facilitate collaboration.

Regularly Revisit and Revise

As market conditions and customer needs evolve, make sure to regularly revisit and update your product vision to ensure it remains relevant and aligned with your business objectives.

Strategy → Tactics

As a PM you get really good at the tactical stuff: requirements gathering, research, sprint management. However, as a Product Leader your role is different. You must think strategically, or you will be hampered when developing a long-term product vision and getting executive buy-in on new product ideas. Thinking strategically is about thinking long term. How does everything fit together, how do the individual smaller features ladder up and complement each other to impact the overarching goals / user needs.

Components of a Game-changing Product Strategy

Product Strategy starts with high level mission and zooms in through vision, strategy, roadmap until you get to on the ground execution.

Mission

  • This is a statement that describes the problem you are setting out to solve, typically including who you are solving it for. This is often set at the company level, but can also be defined for a product or product line.
  • A Mission Statement defines the company’s business, its objectives, and its approach to reach those objectives.
  • Most tech companies have a mission statement.

Example: Google’s mission is “to organize the world’s information and make it universally accessible and useful”.

The mission, by design, is both vague and broad. The further down you go in the stack, the more concrete and narrow these concepts become.

Vision

  • Describes the desired future position of the company. This is the idealized solution that addresses the problem you’ve articulated in your mission.
  • A product vision typically has just enough viability to be relatable, but is still devoid of the details that may hamper your imagination.
  • Product vision is a rallying point for your product — it paints the picture of how your product will make an impact.
  • It is the inspiration and future state we want to achieve.

The vision answers these questions:

  • What is your purpose for creating the product?
  • What positive change should it bring about?

A Template to create your Product Vision:

  • For (the target customers)
  • Who (have a certain need),
  • Our product is a (product category)
  • That provides (compelling reason to buy).
  • Unlike (the product alternative),
  • Our product (has these key differentiators).

When using the above template, one has to be careful to not just describe the status quo but actually make it something worth striving for.

A Vision makes the most sense when it describes customer benefits at a high level, without going into details in how they will be achieved, what the differentiation and market position is etc. This makes the vision future-proof so that it can truly serve as long-term guidance.

Example: Product vision for Yammer, Microsoft’s enterprise social network: “Empower and connect every person across an organization to maximize their impact”.

Some important point to note:

  • Elements of Mission and Vision Statements are often combined to provide a statement of the company’s purposes, goals and values. However, sometimes the two terms are used interchangeably.
  • What is important about the company mission/vision is that it is relatively stable — it should be broad / ambitious enough that it can remain constant for many years.
  • A vision should also be aspirational, or even virtually unattainable, as long as it inspires your team and customers to reach for the new future it describes. Humans are emotional animals — we need inspiring ideas to motivate us. In contrast, a Product Strategy needs to be extremely pragmatic.

Strategy

  • This is a set of principles and decisions — informed by reality and caveated with assumptions — that you commit to ahead of development to ensure the greatest likelihood of success in achieving your vision. Your strategy may evolve with the introduction of new data. If there’s a big shift in strategy while the mission and vision remain constant, it’s called a pivot.
  • In contrast to the vision, which should have a lifetime of many years, a Product Strategy is typically seen to look at a horizon of months to a few years.
  • Strategy describes who your customers are, how your product fits into the current market, and how it will achieve business goals.

Different levels of strategy

Having a more high-level strategy can serve better as an umbrella for all work that is going on in the product organization. Functional strategy is a system of achievable goals and visions that work together to align the team around desirable outcomes for both the business and your customers.

Roadmap

  • This is the manifestation of your strategy in concrete steps towards your product vision, inclusive of rough milestones and timelines. This, too, often changes given new data.
  • The product roadmap describes what products and features will be built to realize the strategy and vision, who is responsible for building those product features, and sometimes an estimate of when those products and features will be released.
  • This is the furthest point you are regularly involved in as a Product Leader. You need to empower your PMs and their teams to own the next level - Execution

Execution

This is the day-to-day activities along the path of the roadmap. This is the PMs on your team do the hard work to build, launch, and iterate, all the while collecting the necessary data to inform any changes in your roadmap and strategy. Your role is to ensure they are empowered and following best practices to increase the likelihood of success

Strategy within this framework bridges the gap between what you aspire to be and what you are doing

Module Summary

Crafting a game-changing Product Strategy requires that it is derived from a clear product vision, prioritized, and then there are ways to measure the outcomes. It starts with your mission - the problem you are setting out to solve. Next follows the vision - the desired future position of the company. Next, the strategy - knowing your customers, your product, and how your product can help fulfil the business goals. Then the final two steps - a concrete roadmap of getting towards the vision and a day-to-day execution plan along the path of the roadmap.

To craft game-changing Product Strategies, you will have to grow and balance your skills as a Product Manager and a Product Leader. As a product manager, one of your most important skills is getting better at tactical stuff such as requirement gathering, research, and spring management. However, as a product leader, you must think strategically to set long-term product vision. You can do this by always being open to discovery, prioritizing, communicating and thinking strategically.
